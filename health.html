WHO Introduces Updated Terminology for Airborne Pathogens in Landmark Consultation

Delhi

20th April, 2024 | 12:30pm

12k

Share:

Due to recent development in airborne pathogen related health risks, Upadtion of airborne pathogen classification became extremely necessary. In a landmark move to streamline terminologies in this area, the World Health Organization (WHO) has unveiled a comprehensive report by collaborating with leading public health agencies and experts worldwide. The publication, titled "Global technical consultation report on proposed terminology for pathogens that transmit through the air," will help actors in the field of health and medicine to understand and educate better about respiratory infections, including COVID-19, influenza, measles, and more.During Covid-19 pandemic the lack of standardized terminology became glaringly evident. Difference in understanding among global stakeholders hindered effective communication and response efforts. To address this challenge, WHO, in collaborated with the Africa Centres for Disease Control and Prevention, the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention, the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control, and the United States Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Together they embarked on a multi-year consultation process spanning 2021-2023.

Dr. Jeremy Farrar, WHO Chief Scientist, put emphasis on the importance of this collaborative effort, stating, "Together with a very diverse range of leading public health agencies and experts across multiple disciplines, we are pleased to have been able to address this complex and timely issue and reach a consensus."The consultation resulted in the introduction of updated descriptors to characterize the transmission of pathogens through the air under typical circumstances. Notably, the term "infectious respiratory particles" (IRPs) was introduced to encompass particles expelled by infected individuals through activities such as breathing, talking, singing, coughing, or sneezing. Additionally, the report emphasizes the continuous spectrum of particle sizes, moving away from the previous dichotomy of "aerosols" and "droplets."

Under the umbrella of "through the air transmission," two describing terms were introduced:
1. Airborne transmission or inhalation: This occurs when IRPs are expelled into the air and inhaled by another person, either at a short or long distance from the infectious individual. Factors such as airflow, humidity, temperature, and ventilation influence the distance at which transmission can occur.
2. Direct deposition: In this scenario, IRPs are expelled into the air and directly deposited on the exposed mouth, nose, or eyes of a nearby individual, potentially leading to infection.

Dr. Gagandeep Kang, Co-Chair of the WHO Technical Working Group, applauded the consensus reached through the consultation process. Dr. Yuguo Li, Co-Chair of the Technical Working Group, echoed his sentiment. The completion of the consultation marks the first phase of global scientific discussions led by WHO. Moving forward, further technical and multidisciplinary research is planned, along with exploration of the wider implementation implications of the updated descriptors.This standardization marks will help foster a common understanding across scientific disciplines and public health sectors, these updated descriptors will play a pivotal role in guiding research agendas, informing policy decisions, and enhancing public communication and response strategies worldwide.
